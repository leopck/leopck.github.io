By early 2020, NVIDIA had two GPU architectures competing for AI workloads: **Volta** (V100, 2017) — purpose-built for datacenter AI — and **Turing** (RTX 2080 Ti, 2018) — designed for consumer graphics with AI acceleration bolted on. Despite Volta being older, it remained the dominant training GPU. Understanding why requires looking at the architectural differences through a performance lens.

The gap widens with model size. ResNet-50 fits comfortably in 11 GB, so Turing keeps up reasonably well (35% slower, matching the bandwidth ratio). BERT-Large's activations strain the 11 GB GDDR6 — batch sizes must be reduced, destroying throughput.

<Callout type="perf" title="Memory Capacity Is the Real Differentiator">
  V100's 16-32 GB HBM2 allows batch sizes 2-3× larger than 2080 Ti's 11 GB. Since training throughput scales nearly linearly with batch size (up to bandwidth saturation), this capacity advantage translates directly to training speed. For models that fit in 11 GB, the performance gap narrows to the bandwidth ratio (~1.46×).
</Callout>

## Architectural Lessons for AI

Several design choices have lasting implications:

**HBM vs GDDR trade-off.** Volta's HBM2 costs more but delivers 46% higher bandwidth per chip. For memory-bound AI workloads (most of inference, large-batch training), this bandwidth directly determines throughput. NVIDIA continued this strategy: A100 and H100 use HBM exclusively for datacenter parts.

**Tensor core evolution.** Turing's 2nd-gen tensor cores added INT8/INT4 support and improved throughput per core. This set the template for Ampere's TF32 and BF16 support — each generation adds new precision modes to broaden the performance envelope.

**Interconnect scaling.** Volta's NVLink advantage at 8 GPUs (7.2× vs 4.8× PCIe) demonstrates why high-bandwidth interconnects are essential for distributed training. This drove NVSwitch (8×NVLink per GPU) in DGX systems and eventually NVLink 4.0 in H100.

## Conclusion

Volta and Turing represent the split between datacenter and consumer GPU design for AI. Volta wins on absolute performance (1.4-1.5× in training) due to HBM bandwidth and memory capacity. Turing wins on price-performance (5-13×) and INT8 inference (1.6×). The memory capacity constraint (11 GB vs 32 GB) is the hard boundary: if your workload fits in 11 GB, Turing offers extraordinary value; if it doesn't, Volta is the only option. This datacenter-vs-consumer tension continues through every subsequent NVIDIA generation.
