Habana Gaudi (acquired by Intel in 2019) entered the AI accelerator market as the first serious alternative to NVIDIA for deep learning training. With a different architecture (graph-compiled TPC kernels vs CUDA), different memory hierarchy (large on-chip SRAM vs L2 cache), and aggressive pricing, Gaudi presented an interesting performance-per-dollar proposition against the established V100.

Graph compilation is Gaudi's strength and weakness. The compiler can optimize memory allocation, operator fusion, and execution scheduling across the entire graph -- often finding optimizations a human CUDA programmer would miss. But it requires the full computation graph upfront, making dynamic models (variable-length sequences, conditional computation) harder to support.

## Limitations and Considerations

**Software ecosystem:** CUDA's ecosystem (cuBLAS, cuDNN, TensorRT, Triton, FlashAttention) is vastly larger. Many optimized kernels don't have Gaudi equivalents, requiring custom TPC kernel development or waiting for SynapseAI updates.

**Dynamic shapes:** Graph compilation struggles with variable-length inputs common in LLM serving. SynapseAI has improved this significantly in later versions with lazy mode and dynamic shape support.

**Custom operations:** Implementing a custom operation on Gaudi (TPC kernel) is harder than writing a CUDA kernel due to the different programming model and limited documentation.

<Callout type="info" title="Looking Forward to Gaudi2/3">
  Gaudi2 (2022) addressed many first-gen limitations: 2.45 TB/s bandwidth, 48 MB SRAM, FP8 support, and improved dynamic shape handling. Gaudi3 pushes further with 4x the AI compute of Gaudi2. The architecture's bandwidth-first design philosophy has aged well as transformer workloads increasingly bottleneck on memory bandwidth.
</Callout>

## Conclusion

Gaudi (1st gen) achieves 88-112% of V100 performance at 2-3x better cost-per-FLOP, with the advantage tilting toward Gaudi for memory-bandwidth-intensive workloads (BERT, transformers) and toward V100 for compute-intensive workloads (ResNet, CNNs). Gaudi's integrated networking eliminates NVSwitch, significantly reducing system cost. The main drawback is the narrower software ecosystem -- CUDA's library advantage remains substantial. For teams with the engineering capacity to work within SynapseAI's constraints, Gaudi offers compelling economics, especially for transformer training at scale.
